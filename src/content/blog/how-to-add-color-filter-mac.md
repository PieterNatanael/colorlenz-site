Your Mac screen is bright. Very bright. And if you spend hours reading, writing, or working through complex information, that brightness works against you in ways that are easy to miss until the end of the day when your eyes feel dry and your focus is gone.

A color filter softens that. It places a subtle tint over your entire display, reducing the harshness of white backgrounds, lowering visual contrast, and shifting the color temperature toward something easier to sustain.

Here is how to add one to your Mac.

---

## The Built-In Option: macOS Color Filters

Apple includes a color filter feature in macOS, tucked inside Accessibility settings. Here is how to find it:

1. Open **System Settings** (the gear icon in your Dock or Apple menu)
2. Go to **Accessibility**
3. Select **Display**
4. Scroll to **Color Filters** and toggle it on
5. Choose a filter type from the list

macOS offers five filter types:

- **Grayscale** — removes all color from the display
- **Red/Green Filter** — for red-green color blindness
- **Green/Red Filter** — alternative for red-green color blindness
- **Blue/Yellow Filter** — for blue-yellow color blindness
- **Color Tint** — applies a single color wash you can adjust with a hue slider

For accessibility use cases like color blindness, these filters are well suited. For general screen comfort, focus, or eye strain reduction, they are limited. You get five presets, one color wheel, and no ability to adjust brightness, contrast, saturation, temperature, or how intensely the filter applies.

You can also set a keyboard shortcut to toggle the filter on and off:

1. In the same **Accessibility > Display** section, look for **Keyboard Shortcut**
2. Enable it to toggle color filters with **Option + Command + F**

## The Other Built-In Tools

macOS has a few other display adjustment features worth knowing:

**Night Shift** (System Settings > Displays > Night Shift) shifts the screen toward warmer colors on a schedule. It reduces blue light in the evening but runs automatically by time of day and has limited manual control.

**True Tone** (on supported Macs) adjusts the display based on ambient light. It is passive and cannot be tuned.

**Reduce White Point** (Accessibility > Display) dims the white areas of your screen. It helps with brightness but does not change color tone.

None of these give you precise, real-time control over how your screen looks and feels across different types of work.

If you have color blindness or need a macOS-level filter for accessibility reasons, the built-in Color Filters are the right place to start. They work at the system level and do not require a third-party app.
