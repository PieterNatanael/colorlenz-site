The MacBook Neo is a genuinely exciting Mac. At $599, it brings a full Apple Silicon experience to a price point that makes sense for students, first-time Mac buyers, and anyone who has been waiting for the right moment to switch. The A18 Pro chip, the build quality, the color options — it delivers where it counts.

Once you have it set up, one of the first things worth getting right is your display. You will be looking at this screen for hours every day. A few minutes of setup now makes a real difference over time.

Here is everything worth knowing.

---

## Start With Brightness

**System Settings > Displays > Brightness**

The most immediate adjustment. The MacBook Neo's screen is bright by default, which is great for outdoor use but more than you need indoors.

Turn on **Automatically adjust brightness** if you want the Mac to handle this for you — the ambient light sensor adjusts the backlight based on your environment. Most people find this works well and forget about it.

If you prefer manual control, set brightness to somewhere between 50 and 70 percent for typical indoor use. You can always bump it up when you step outside.

---

## Night Shift

**System Settings > Displays > Night Shift**

Night Shift shifts the display toward warmer, amber tones in the evening. It is based on research showing that cooler blue light in the hours before bed can affect sleep quality. The Mac transitions gradually at sunset and returns to normal at sunrise.

Turn it on and set the warmth to your preference. Most people land somewhere in the middle of the slider. If you work late regularly, this is one of the most useful settings on any Mac.

You can also trigger it manually with a toggle if you want it on during the day.

---

## Reduce White Point

**System Settings > Accessibility > Display > Reduce White Point**

This one is less known but very useful. Standard brightness dims the whole backlight. Reduce White Point specifically targets the bright white areas in app interfaces — the white backgrounds in Safari, Mail, Notes, and most productivity apps.

If your screen feels harsh in a dark room even at low brightness, enabling this and dragging the slider to around 30 to 40 percent makes a noticeable difference. It is especially good for late-night sessions.

---

## Color Filters

**System Settings > Accessibility > Display > Color Filters**

macOS includes a set of full-screen color filters, primarily designed for users with color blindness. The options include grayscale, red-green and blue-yellow adjustments, and a Color Tint mode with a hue slider.

The Color Tint option is worth trying if you want a soft overlay in a specific tone. You can set a keyboard shortcut (Option + Command + F) to toggle it on and off without going back into Settings.

---

## A Note on the Neo's Display

The MacBook Neo uses a 13-inch sRGB display calibrated for everyday use. Apple designed the Neo for a specific buyer — someone who wants a capable, well-built Mac at an accessible price — and the display reflects those priorities. It is sharp, accurate for sRGB content, and comfortable for most tasks.

The higher-end MacBook Air and MacBook Pro lines include True Tone, which uses sensors to adapt the display's color temperature to the ambient light in the room. The Neo does not have this feature.

For many users that is not noticeable at all. For others, particularly those who work under warm indoor lighting and find the screen looks cooler than they would like, it is something worth addressing manually.

## Going Further: A Free Screen Filter

If you want the kind of real-time display tuning that goes beyond what the built-in settings offer — adjusting warmth, brightness, saturation, and color tone together, saving those combinations as named presets, and switching between them instantly — that is where a dedicated screen filter comes in.

[Goodmood](https://apps.apple.com/id/app/goodmood-screen-companion/id6765573775?mt=12) is a free macOS screen filter that runs as a transparent floating overlay above all your apps. It works on the MacBook Neo and every other Mac, and it complements the built-in settings rather than replacing them. Night Shift still handles your evening warmth. Goodmood handles the finer tuning on top.

What it adds:

- Independent control over temperature, brightness, saturation, contrast, and color tone
- Named presets you can switch between from the menu bar
- Privacy Mode: one tap to cover your screen when someone is nearby
- Works with every app — browsers, editors, Claude AI, Notion, video players, everything

The app is free to download. The core filter and Privacy Mode work with no subscription. Custom presets and additional built-in presets are behind the $0.99/month premium tier.

## The Short Version

For most MacBook Neo owners, this setup covers everything:

1. Enable **Automatically adjust brightness**
2. Turn on **Night Shift** on a sunset schedule
3. Enable **Reduce White Point** at around 30 percent if you work in dim environments
4. Add [Goodmood](https://apps.apple.com/id/app/goodmood-screen-companion/id6765573775?mt=12) if you want real-time warmth and tone control beyond what the built-in settings offer

The Neo is a great Mac. A few minutes of display setup makes it a comfortable one for the long sessions it was built for.
